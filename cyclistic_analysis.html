Phase 1

Ask

Define the business task and identify key stakeholders before touching any data.

Business Task Statement

The single question this analysis answers
How do annual members and casual riders use Cyclistic bikes differently? Cyclistic's finance team has determined that annual members are significantly more profitable than casual riders. Director of Marketing Lily Moreno has tasked the analytics team with identifying behavioral differences between these two groups. The findings will inform a targeted digital marketing campaign designed to convert existing casual riders — who already know the product — into annual members.

import pandas as pd import glob # Load all 12 monthly files files = sorted(glob.glob("data/2025*.csv") + glob.glob("data/2026*.csv")) dfs = [pd.read_csv(f, parse_dates= ['started_at','ended_at']) for f in files] df = pd.concat(dfs, ignore_index=True) # → 5,848,703 rows # Derive ride length in minutes df['ride_length_min'] = ( df['ended_at'] - df['started_at'] ).dt.total_seconds() / 60 # Remove invalid durations (step 3 & 4) df = df[(df['ride_length_min'] > 0) & (df['ride_length_min'] < 1440)] # → 5,842,831 rows (−5,872 bad rides) # Derive time features df['day_of_week'] = df['started_at'].dt.day_name() df['month'] = df['started_at'].dt.to_period('M') df['hour'] = df['started_at'].dt.hour

Finding 1 — Casuals Take Fewer But Much Longer Rides Members average 12.1 minutes per ride; casuals average 18.9 minutes. The median tells the same story (8.6 vs 11.2 min). This 56% gap in average duration — combined with casuals being most active on weekends — strongly suggests casual riders use bikes for leisure and exploration, while members use them for short, predictable commutes.

Finding 2 — Members Are Commuters; Casuals Are Weekenders Member rides peak Tuesday–Thursday (classic commuter signature) and decline sharply on weekends. Casual rides climb steadily through the week, peaking Saturday with 431,737 rides — their busiest day. On Saturday, casuals account for 48% of all rides, nearly matching members. This binary behavioral pattern is one of the strongest signals in the dataset.

Finding 3 — Members Show a Classic Commuter Demand Curve Member ride volume spikes at 8am (273,069 rides) and 5pm (404,857 rides) — textbook rush hour. Casual volume has no morning spike; it climbs from midday and peaks in the late afternoon (5pm: 200,633) then fades. This confirms: members are largely commuting; casuals are largely choosing to ride when it suits their leisure schedule.

4.4 — Seasonal Trends

Monthly Ride Volume

Finding 4 — Casuals Are More Seasonally Volatile In August 2025 (peak), casuals took 337,246 rides. By January 2026, that collapsed to 24,656 — a 92.7% drop. Members went from 452,355 (Aug) to 112,933 (Jan) — a 75% drop. The deeper seasonal swing in casual ridership suggests they treat cycling as optional warm-weather recreation, while members integrate it as year-round transportation.

What the Data Tells Us About Casual Riders

Profiling the conversion target
They're Experienced Riders They already know the product, trust the service, and complete 35.9% of all trips. The conversion barrier is pricing psychology, not product awareness.
They Ride for Pleasure Long trips from lakefront leisure stations on weekends suggest they value the experience of riding, not the efficiency. This is a marketing hook.
They're Weather-Sensitive The 93% winter drop means campaigns launched in spring (April–May) will reach them right as they're returning — highest receptivity window.
They Prefer Electric 68.7% of casual rides use electric bikes vs 65.8% for members. An "electric-first" membership pitch aligns with their revealed preference.
Phase 6

Act

Three concrete, data-backed recommendations for Lily Moreno and the Cyclistic executive team.

01

Launch a "Weekend Warrior" Membership Tier

Casuals ride overwhelmingly on weekends (Saturday is their peak day with 431,737 rides). A discounted weekend-only membership — priced between a day pass and a full annual membership — directly addresses the value equation for this segment. Price it so 8+ weekend rides per month make it cheaper than individual passes. Promote via ads at the top-10 casual stations (Navy Pier, Millennium Park, Lake Shore Dr) during peak spring/summer weekends.

02

Spring Conversion Campaign (April–June)

Casual ridership drops 93% in winter, then rebounds sharply in spring. April–June is the moment of highest intent: riders are re-engaging after months off and making decisions about the season. Target these returning casuals with a limited-time spring membership promotion ("Ride All Summer For Less"). Serve digital ads on the Cyclistic app when a casual rider completes their 3rd ride of the season — the data suggests they're already committed for the warm months.

03

In-App "Your Cost Breakdown" Feature

The most effective conversion tool is showing casual riders exactly how much they've spent on single passes vs. what a membership would cost. Build a personalized in-app screen that shows: "You've taken X rides this year, spending ~$Y. An annual membership would have cost $Z — saving you $W." Trigger this push notification after a rider's 5th casual ride. Casuals take longer rides (18.9 min avg), meaning each trip costs them more per use — this calculation will be compelling when made concrete.
